## Design Context

### Users
- **Primary audience**: Privacy-conscious users who value control over their personal data
- **Context**: People who are aware of surveillance capitalism and want alternatives to cloud-based services
- **Job to be done**: Find information they've seen before without sacrificing privacy or relying on external servers
- **Technical literacy**: Likely higher-than-average; comfortable with browser extensions and local-first architecture

### Brand Personality
- **Voice**: Sharp, intelligent, precise
- **Tone**: Confident but not arrogant; technical but accessible
- **3-word personality**: Sharp / Smart / Trustworthy
- **Emotional goals**: Instill confidence in data security; feel empowering (user is in control); signal technical sophistication without being elitist

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Visual tone**: Surgical precision meets understated elegance
- **References to consider**:
  - Linear (crisp typography, purposeful motion)
  - Obsidian (knowledge-tool aesthetic, dark by default)
  - Apple Design (refined details, restraint in decoration)
- **Anti-references** (what to avoid):
  - Generic SaaS startup aesthetic (purple gradients, Inter font, glassmorphism)
  - Playful/cute interfaces (not appropriate for security/privacy context)
  - Overly corporate/bland (needs personality)
- **Theme**: Dark mode primary (privacy/security association); light mode optional
- **Color notes**: Avoid the "AI palette" (cyan/blue/purple on dark). Consider neutral with a single confident accent.

### Design Principles

1. **Privacy is the feature, not the aesthetic** — Don't use privacy tropes (locks, shields as primary visuals). Let the functionality speak for itself.

2. **Every pixel earns its place** — Sharp/Smart means no decorative fluff. No border beams, no gradient text, no glassmorphism. Purposeful only.

3. **Speed implies safety** — Fast animations, instant feedback. When data is local, performance is a trust signal.

4. **Typography is the personality** — Since we avoid decorative elements, type choice and hierarchy carry the design. Distinctive but readable.

5. **Clarity over cleverness** — Privacy tools can't afford confusion. Labels should be obvious, flows should be predictable.

### Priorities (in order)
1. User experience (must feel effortless)
2. Design (distinctive, memorable)
3. Usefulness (solves real problems)
